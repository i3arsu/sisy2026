§ 02 — About Pula

A city between Rome and the sea.

Pula is the largest city in Istria, the heart-shaped peninsula at the northern end of the Adriatic. Founded by the Histri and later a Roman colony, Pula preserves one of the best-surviving Roman amphitheaters in the world — built in the 1st century AD and still used today for concerts and screenings during the summer.

Around the arena, a compact city core layers Roman, Venetian, Habsburg, and modern Croatian architecture. The seafront promenade (Lungomare) stretches south from the harbor toward forested capes and protected swimming coves.

In late September, the Adriatic is calm, the sea is still warm enough for swimming, and the busy summer crowds have left. Average daytime temperatures hover around 22–24 °C, and the sea is typically 21–23 °C — ideal conditions for a working symposium and a few afternoons by the water.

Pula is the seat of Juraj Dobrila University, founded in 2006 and named for the 19th-century Istrian bishop and patron of Croatian culture. The Faculty of Economics and Tourism is one of its newest, with strong programs in tourism, management, and informatics.

§ 04 — Getting there

How to reach Pula.

Pula is well-connected by air, road, and rail across Europe. Most international visitors arrive via Pula Airport or via Zagreb, Trieste, or Venice.

№ 01 / By air

Pula Airport (PUY)

Direct seasonal flights from major European hubs including Frankfurt, London, Manchester, Munich, and Amsterdam. The airport sits ~10 km from the city center; shuttle and taxi service available.

~10 km · 15 min
№ 02 / Via Zagreb

Through the capital

Zagreb Airport (ZAG) is well-connected year-round. From Zagreb, take an intercity bus (~4 hrs) or rent a car for a scenic drive through the Učka tunnel into Istria.

~270 km · 3.5 hrs
№ 03 / Via Italy

From Trieste or Venice

Trieste Airport is ~2.5 hours away by bus; Venice Marco Polo is ~4 hours. Both are practical options for visitors connecting through major European airlines.

§ 06 — Around town

Things to see & do.

If you arrive a day early or stay on after the symposium, Pula and the surrounding Istrian peninsula reward exploration.

Pula Arena
1st-century Roman amphitheater — one of the six largest surviving in the world. Self-guided visits daily; evening events in summer.
The Forum
The Roman main square, dominated by the Temple of Augustus (2 BC – 14 AD). Cafés, gelato, and the city's quiet heart.
Lungomare
The seafront promenade winding south from the harbor along forested capes and protected coves — walking, swimming, sunset views.
Cape Kamenjak
A wild nature park at the southern tip of Istria, ~30 minutes by car. Cliffs, dinosaur footprints, and crystal water.
Brijuni Islands
A national park archipelago a short ferry from Fažana — Roman ruins, a safari park, and Tito-era history.
Rovinj & Motovun
Two of Istria's most photographed towns — Rovinj on the coast, Motovun in the hill country famous for truffles and wine.
Istrian cuisine
Truffles, olive oil, fresh Adriatic seafood, and Istrian Malvazija wine. Many restaurants within a short walk of the venue.
